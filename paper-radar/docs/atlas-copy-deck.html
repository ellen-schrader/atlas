We've been selling the filing cabinet.

Every version of this copy has sold Atlas as a better place to put papers — import them, search them, map them, keep track of what you've read. That's the mechanism, not the product. The product is that a lab has a taste and a look, both of which currently live only in people's heads — and Atlas is the first thing that writes them down and hands them to Claude.

The strategic shift

The reading tools are not the offer. They're how the offer gets built. Every save, reaction, comment and figure upload is a lesson — so using Atlas is training Atlas. That single idea resolves the tension we've been stuck on: we don't have to choose between "useful on day one" and "taste is the point". Day one is the first lesson.

What we were saying
“One home for everything your lab reads.”

True, and commodity. Zotero, Mendeley, Paperpile and a shared Drive folder all say this. It gives a PI no reason to move, and it makes the taste model sound like a bonus feature.

What we should say
“Your lab's taste is its most valuable asset. Atlas is where it lives.”

Judgment — what's worth reading, what's worth arguing with, what a good figure looks like — is the scarce thing a lab actually owns. Nobody else is selling it, and it's the only story that makes the MCP integration inevitable rather than a feature.

Four territories

Each is a complete, self-consistent way to sell Atlas — one-liner, hero, and the section headlines beneath it. They're mutually exclusive as a headline but they borrow from each other in the body. My recommendation is №2, with lines from №4 used as supporting copy.

1Institutional memory most emotional

When the postdoc leaves, the taste stays.

Five years of judgment about what's worth reading walks out of your lab with every person who finishes. Atlas keeps it — the papers, the arguments, the figures you admired — so the next person inherits the lab's mind, not just its Dropbox.

The recommendation

Lead with №2, resolve cold start with №4, close with №1.

Hero: “Every lab has a taste. Atlas gives yours to Claude.” — the only claim no competitor can make. Immediately beneath it: “It learns by being used” (№4), which turns the empty taste model from an objection into the reason to start today. At the bottom of the page, before the CTA: “When the postdoc leaves, the taste stays” (№1) — the emotional close, once they already understand the mechanism. Territory 3 becomes the About page.

The one thing this copy must not do

Promise that Claude “knows your lab” on day one. It doesn't — the taste vector is empty until people engage, and a PI who tests that claim in week one and finds it hollow will never come back. Every line above is written to promise a trajectory, not an instant. “It learns” is a sellable claim. “It knows” is a lie with a 30-day fuse.

Voice

We're talking to working scientists, who are allergic to marketing and can smell an overclaim from the abstract. The voice is a smart colleague explaining something, not a vendor.

Do

Be specific about the mechanism.“Learns from what you save and react to” beats “AI-powered personalisation”.
Say “your lab”, not “users” or “teams”.The lab is the unit of identity. It's also how they refer to themselves.
Promise a trajectory, not a miracle.“It learns” / “it gets sharper” — verifiable, and it survives week one.
Name the real objects.A .bib file. A palette. A matplotlib stylesheet. Concrete nouns build trust.

Don't

AI-powered supercharge revolutionise seamlessInstant credibility loss with this audience.
Claude knows your labIt doesn't, until you've used it. Say learns.
Never miss a paper againAn unkeepable promise, and it sells anxiety.
Your second brain knowledge graphCategory clichés. They make us sound like every other tool.
